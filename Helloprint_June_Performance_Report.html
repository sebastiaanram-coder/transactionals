Performance Review · June 2026

The June slowdown is a traffic problem,
not a conversion problem.

Orders softened in the second half of June. The cause is fewer visitors reaching the site — not a drop in how well those visitors convert. Conversion actually improved. The lever is traffic recovery, not the funnel, pricing, or checkout.

01What happened

Orders drifted down through the month

Daily order volume held strong through the first week of June, then stepped down week after week. By the final full week, weekday orders were running roughly a fifth below where the month started — a steady decline rather than a one-off dip.

02The cause

It's traffic, not conversion

If conversion were the problem, orders would have fallen faster than visits. The opposite happened: visits fell 26% while orders fell 20%, so the share of visitors who placed an order actually rose from 8.3% to 9.0%.

Fewer people arrived — but the ones who did bought at least as well as before.

This pattern, measured across the 50 highest-traffic products, points squarely at the top of the funnel: acquisition. The on-site experience that turns a visit into an order is working fine.

Conclusion: the order decline is fully explained by lost traffic. Conversion is healthy, so optimising the funnel, discounting, or changing checkout would not address the real issue.

Conversion held at the product level too

Looking product by product across the top sellers, the same picture holds: conversion is mostly flat or higher in the soft window than in the strong one. A couple of products softened, but none collapsed — there is no product whose funnel broke.

03Where

The traffic loss is broad — most markets, both labels

This isn't one country or one shop. Traffic is down across nearly every market, with France and the Netherlands carrying the largest absolute loss. The UK and Germany held up better; the US even grew. Both Endlabel and Connect are affected, which points to a shared, top-of-funnel cause rather than a single-market issue.

04Who

New customers are hit hardest

New-customer orders fell 38% while returning-customer orders fell 14%. New customers depend almost entirely on fresh traffic reaching the site; loyal customers reorder directly. The fact that acquisition dropped nearly three times as fast as repeat business is the clearest fingerprint of a traffic problem.

05What this means

Focus on getting traffic back

  • Treat this as an acquisition issue. The decline is driven by visitors not arriving — the priority is restoring top-of-funnel traffic, not changing on-site conversion.
  • Start with France and the Netherlands. They account for the largest share of the lost visits and orders.
  • Check what changed in mid-June across the channels that bring new visitors (paid and organic acquisition, landing entry points), since the drop begins there and hits new customers hardest.
  • Leave the funnel alone for now. Conversion, pricing and checkout are performing well; they are not where the orders were lost.
